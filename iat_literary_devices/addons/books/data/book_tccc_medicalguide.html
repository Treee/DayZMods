Purpose

Tactical Combat Casualty Care (TCCC) teaches how to treat injuries when help isn’t coming. Keep yourself and your team alive long enough to reach proper care.


Phases of Care

There are three main phases: Care Under Fire, Tactical Field Care, Evacuation Care.


Care Under Fire

Stay in the fight. Return fire and move to cover. Shout for the wounded to crawl or find cover if able.

Only treat life-threatening bleeding. Apply a tourniquet high and tight using belt, band, or strap.


Tactical Field Care

Once safe, start a full assessment. Check breathing, bleeding, and responsiveness.

Open the airway. For chest wounds with sucking sounds, seal with plastic or tape.


Bleeding Control

Use direct pressure or a tourniquet. If the first tourniquet fails, apply a second above it. Pack deep wounds with cloth and maintain pressure.


Airway & Breathing

If unconscious but breathing, place casualty on side to prevent choking. If breathing stops, start rescue breaths only if safe.


Circulation & Shock

Keep the casualty warm. Shock kills quietly. Use jacket, tarp, or blanket to retain heat.


Evacuation Care

Prepare casualty for movement. Re-check bandages and tourniquets. Record what you did — tourniquet times, wounds treated, meds if any — and pass info along.


Priorities for Evac

Secure casualty — prevent further harm. Monitor breathing continuously. Control bleeding before transport. Keep warm and hydrated.


Improvised Tools

Tourniquet: Belt, torn clothing, rope. Pressure bandage: Shirt, rag, scarf. Chest seal: Plastic wrap, tape, bag. Splint: Stick, board, rifle barrel.


First Aid Tips

Talk to the casualty — it helps keep them conscious. Reassess every few minutes. Stop bleeding and keep them breathing.


Environmental Awareness

Move casualty out of exposed areas when possible. Avoid cold, wet, or unsafe terrain. Medical priorities come first; equipment is secondary.


Prioritize Life over Loot

Medical priorities come first. Stop bleeding and maintain airway before gathering supplies.


Final Notes

“You are the medic until one arrives.”

Stay calm. Keep casualty breathing, warm, and monitored until evacuation or help arrives.
